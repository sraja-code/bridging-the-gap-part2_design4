Chapter One

Introduction

You are about to write for a student you will probably never meet face to face. That single fact is behind almost everything in this guidebook.

In a lecture hall, you can read the room — a puzzled expression, a raised hand, a shuffle of feet that tells you it's time to move on. At the Open University of Sri Lanka, that feedback loop doesn't exist in real time. The material you write has to do the noticing for you: anticipate the question before it's asked, spot where a learner is likely to stall, and keep them moving without a teacher standing beside them.

That's the idea this book keeps returning to, whichever format you happen to be working in. Whether you're drafting a print module, scripting a short video, building an activity in Moodle, or asking a generative AI tool for a first pass at some content, the same test applies: does this material stand in for a teacher who isn't there?

The chapters ahead work through what that means in practice — starting with the principles of Open and Distance Learning, moving through the formats OUSL uses to reach its students, and finishing with a practical look at how generative AI fits into that process without taking over from your own judgement.

What to expect
  • Chapters Two to Four set out the thinking behind self-instructional material
  • Chapters Five to Seven cover print, audio-visual, and LMS-based formats in turn
  • Chapter Eight is a hands-on guide to using generative AI responsibly in your writing

Chapter Two

Principles and Practices of Open and Distance Learning

Distance education has travelled a long journey, continuously improving as a method of teaching and learning. Distance learners have neither a regular classroom nor teachers to create an environment for learning; instructional materials take on that role.

At OUSL, self-learning materials are the main medium of instruction. The teacher is, in effect, built into the material, performing the tasks a classroom teacher would: presenting content, creating interaction, and motivating the learner. Because learners study at their own pace and place, course development is a rigorous, systematic process.

Self-learning materials, in essence
  • Written primarily for the learner, not the teacher
  • Always state aims and learning outcomes
  • Designed for a particular learner group
  • Place major emphasis on self-assessment

Self-learning vs. conventional materials

TextbooksSelf-Learning Materials
Assume interestArouse interest

Chapter Three

Modes of ODL Delivery and Multimodal Learning

As an academic working within the ODL system, your job isn't really to "deliver content" — it's to design an experience for a student who is studying alone, at a distance, often late at night after a full day of work. Print-based self-instructional material still does the heavy lifting here, but it rarely works alone anymore. Good delivery today usually means choosing two or three modes and making them talk to each other.

Used in combination
  • Print SIMs — the core, a “teacher in print”
  • Audio — useful on the move and for auditory learners
  • Video — to demonstrate and create teacher presence
  • LMS (Moodle) — resources, quizzes, and discussion
  • Multimodal materials — text, visuals, audio, activity integrated
  • Open Educational Resources — to enrich and save time

The key idea is integration, not addition: a lesson might introduce a concept in text, demonstrate it with a short video, and reinforce it with an activity, rather than posting a PDF, a video, and a quiz with no connection between them.

Key message
  • No single delivery mode works for everyone
  • Effective ODL programmes combine modes, tailored to learners and outcomes
Chapter Four

Designing and Developing Multimodal Instructional Materials

Good study material rarely arrives fully formed. It moves through a fairly predictable sequence — from a rough outline down to a finished, teachable unit — with feedback pulling you back a step or two along the way. Knowing that sequence in advance saves you from the two most common traps: writing content before you've settled the outcomes, and polishing a session before you know whether the framework around it actually holds up.

The development cycle
  • Identify the needs of the target group
  • Clarify aims and learning outcomes
  • Develop a framework for the course
  • Develop the study sessions
  • Compile the sessions into a unit

Role of the educational technologist

This is where an educational technologist earns their keep. They're not there to write your content for you — they're there to pressure-test it: is the structure sound, do the outcomes actually match the activities, and would a learner seeing this cold know what to do next? A short conversation with one, early, tends to save a lot of rework later.

Chapter Five

Developing Print-Based Instructional Materials

Are you aware of the foremost service expected of an academic employed in the Open and Distance Learning (ODL) system? It is designing and developing self instructional material (SIMs) for their distance learners (Jayakody, 2022).

Figure 4.1: An Open and Distance Learning (ODL) student studying self-instructional materials at a desk [AI-generated image]. https://gemini.google.com/

In the ODL system, SIMs make up the most critical component for delivering education. It is not merely a textbook; it is a meticulously designed learning resource that replaces the physical presence and immediate interaction with the teacher. The core expectation from SIMs is that they must fulfill the multifaceted roles of a traditional classroom teacher.

Let’s first recollect the role of a traditional classroom teacher and that of a SIM.

Key Characteristics and Expectations of SIMs

In an ODL context, the material must be designed to be truly self-sufficient and interactive. However, before we move any further, let’s look at this video and get an idea on the main features of SIMs.

Self-Help Activity 4.1 (Based on the above video)
  • If you have designed and developed SIMs for your distance learners before, did you include these features in them?
  • Have you noticed these features in SIMs written by other OUSL academics?

So, for an OUSL SIM to effectively support the ODL process, it must embody the following characteristics (Bates, 2019).

  • Self-Pacing: The material must allow the learner to progress at their own speed, providing both remediation (extra help) and enrichment (additional challenges) within the text.
  • Self-Contained: It must contain all the necessary information, examples, and activities required to achieve the learning outcomes of that unit. External references should be supplementary, not essential.
  • Active Learning Focus: SIMs should use techniques like embedded questions, tasks, and reflections to constantly require the student to do something with the information, not just passively read it.
  • Clarity and Language: The language must be clear, concise, and accessible. Complex concepts must be broken down and explained using appropriate analogies and examples relevant to the Sri Lankan context where possible.
  • Structure and Signposting: Effective use of headings, bolding, bullet points, numbering, and margin notes ensures the learner can easily navigate the material and identify key takeaways.

As evidenced, the design and development of these comprehensive and self-sufficient SIMs is therefore not just a task, but the foremost service expected from an academic employed in the ODL system (as mentioned previously). It is the primary means by which you, as the subject matter expert, reach and teach your distant students.

Foundations of ODL and SIMs

As academics of the Open University of Sri Lanka (OUSL), we need to have an understanding of the difference between writing a common textbook and creating a dynamic instructional tool, which highlights the fundamentals of the “why” and “how” of ODL.

ODL is a multi-dimensional approach to education that breaks the traditional mould and is built on two distinct pillars: "Open" philosophy and "Distance" reality. "Openness" refers to the removal of barriers that prevent people from learning, with open entry (minimal or no formal entry requirements), open place (learning happens at home, workplace, or on the move), and open pace (learners decide how fast or slow they want to progress).

Figure 4.2: The "Open" Philosophy - Removing Barriers to Learning [AI-generated image]. https://gemini.google.com

"Distance", on the other hand, refers to the separation between the teacher and learner in space and/or time. Since the teacher is not physically present to clarify a confusing paragraph or answer a sudden question, the printed material must take over the teacher’s role.

Before designing and developing such print material, you must understand the classical theories of distance education that govern ODL. They will help you to tell apart the concept of “distance education” from the theory and practice of correspondence studies, which emerged in the nineteenth century (Fritsch, 2001).

Otto Peters, Börje Holmberg, and Michael Moore are three classical theorists who have been influential in both understanding and practising distance education, and the following table comprehensively presents the respective theories they put forward, their characteristics, and the implementation of their theoretical understanding.

Table 4.2
Characteristic of Classical Distance Education Theories
Otto Peters (1973)Börje Holmberg (1983)Michael Moore (1993)
TheoryTheory of IndustrialisationGuided Didactic Conversation TheoryTransactional Distance Theory
Main characteristicsDivision of work among cooperating individuals, mechanisation of material production and dissemination, and planning and mass production.
  • Communication leading to guided didactic conversation.
  • Emphasis on one-to-one relationship between tutor and learner in ODL.
  • In ODL, responsibility for teaching lies with course designers
  • Learning experience is determined by a learning activity.
  • Distinct relationship between learner and facilitator in a learning process
  • Learner autonomy in shaping their aims, objectives, and assessment and evaluation parameters.

  • Distance education as a standardised educational mass system.
  • Similar to industrialised production processes.
  • Course designed incorporating a conversational tone into the content of the course.
  • Dialogue created between learner and teacher, through learning materials.
  • Applying appropriate instructional design features to bridge the communication gap between facilitator and learner caused by distance.

In a nutshell, the Theory of Industrialisation focuses on treating course production like a high-quality assembly line (writing, editing, printing); the Guided Didactic Conversation Theory, on the idea of text "talking" to the student, using a conversational tone ("Now, try this...") instead of a formal one; and the Transactional Distance Theory, on the learners’ ability to take charge of their own study, providing clear instructions so that they don’t feel lost."

The ODL Learner

It is a must that we get to know who an ODL learner is before we cater to him/her with SIM’s.

So, who is an ODL learner?

An ODL learner is typically an adult, often juggling work, family, and study. Unlike conventional students, they don’t just consume knowledge; they try to fit it into the gaps of their busy life schedules. They are self-directed and prefer to take charge of their own learning journey (Knowles, 1980). Also, they come with years of professional and life experience, which they use to test the validity of what you write in the SIMs. Apart from that, they use these SIMs as a primary connection to the institution, in your case, the Open University of Sri Lanka (OUSL), since most of them are prone to studying in isolation.

The following table will give you a clearer idea of an ODL learner’s profile.

Table 4.3

FeatureDescription

Goal orientedThey are usually studying for a specific reason (i.e., career advancement, personal fulfillment).

Being aware of these facts is important to you as ODL academics, as the ODL learner is physically separated from you, and the SIM must act as a "Teacher in Print." It must anticipate their questions, provide the encouragement a tutor would give in person, and bridge the gap between theory and their real-world practice.

Writing to the Distance Learner

Now that you have a general idea of what SIMs must inherently have as their essential features, we shall now elaborate on designing and developing SIMs to best suit our distance learners so that they reap the maximum benefits in their learning process. In this process, language plays a significant role in bridging the gap between you and the learner, and the following sections of this chapter will inform you how language can be manipulated to best suit the ODL system with appropriate examples.

Why must we bridge the gap?

Attending interactive learning sessions (ILS) is not a must.
https://gemini.google.com/

Your physical presence, gestures, and tone of voice must be replaced.

The voice of SIMs

It is extremely important that you use plain language because of the linguistic situation of your target group, since the language you use should be able to retain your learner’s attention. Basically, the material you write must be highly readable.

So, how can you make the SIMs you write user-friendly?

Adopt a conversational tone.

Figure 4.3: Connecting the writer’s tone with reader’s mood through conversational style [AI-genereated image]. https://gemini.google.com/

Use personal pronouns

Say "you" for the learner and "we/I" for the teacher.

Avoid: “The reader is requested to recall how the topic of Photosynthesis was discussed in the previous session by the author.”

Instead: “You’ll remember how I mentioned photosynthesis in the previous session.”

Address the learner directly

When you are addressing your learner, move away from third person and passive voice.

Avoid: “Students are required to observe the following diagram. It is expected that the reader will identify three main components of the cell. One must ensure that the differences between plant and animal cells are noted before the next chapter is commenced.”

The above sort of writing creates a barrier and lacks personal connection.

Instead: “Now, I would like you to take a close look at this diagram. What do you notice first? As you study the image, try to pick out the three main components of the cell. Once you feel confident in identifying them, move on to the next section where we will explore the differences between plant and animal cells.”

Table 4.4
Key Rules for Direct Addressing in SIMs
FeatureAvoid (traditional)Use (self-instructional)
PerspectiveThird person (“the learner”)Second person (“you”)
VoicePassive (“it is suggested ….”)Active (“I suggest you ……”)
ToneFormal/distantConversational/warm
GuidanceStatement of factAsking questions and giving directions

Use contractions where necessary

Using contractions is one of the most effective ways to make the material feel like a spoken conversation between the teacher and learner.

Table 4.5
Contractions That Could Be Used in SIMs
Traditional textbook styleODL “guided conversational” style
“Do not worry if ….”“Don’t worry if ….”
“It is likely that ….”“It’s likely that ….”
“We are now ready to ….”“We’re now ready to …..”
“You will see that ….”“You’ll see that ….”

However, words like “shouldn’t’ve” (should not have) are too informal and can be hard for non-native speakers to decode. So, stick to simple ones like don’t, it’s, we’re, and you’ll.

Also, if a point is absolutely critical or a safety warning, sometimes the full form is better for emphasis (e.g., "Do not open the battery case" is stronger than "Don't open...").

Choice of words

Try avoiding surplus words because there must be clarity over clutter.

  • “at the present moment in time” ……………………. now
  • “with a view to/in order to” ……………………… to
  • “owing to the fact that” ………………………….. because
  • “on the grounds that” …………………………. because

Specialist vocabulary

There are certain ‘do’s and ‘don’t’s to remember when using specialist vocabulary.

  • Don’t use technical terms unnecessarily, unless they are essential.
  • Explain carefully when you introduce them.
  • Remind the reader of their meaning at appropriate times.
  • You could even use a glossary.

Sentence structure

  • Make them short and simple.
  • Use short sentences: aim for 15-20 words
  • Have one idea per paragraph.
  • Use familiar words: Choose “use" instead of “utilise," or “show" instead of “demonstrate."

Avoid: “The fact that the molecules in a liquid are closer together and thus the attractive forces between them are very much greater than the very minute forces between the molecules of a gas, is responsible for the greater resistance by a liquid to compression.”

Instead: “The molecules in a liquid are closer together than they are in a gas. As a result, the attractive forces between the liquid molecules are much greater than the very minute forces between the gas molecules. Therefore, liquids are more resistant to comprehension than gases.”

Use active voice and not passive

Table 4.6
Active Voice Counterparts of Passive Voice That Should Be Used in SIMs
Passive (Avoid)Active (Use)
"The experiment should be conducted by the student now."“Now, conduct the experiment.”
“Metal beams are corroded by salt water.”“Salt water corrodes metal beams.”
“In this section, the questions will be viewed analytically.“Now, we’ll analyse the questions.”
“The reader is asked to draw a map of his/her town/village.”“You could draw a map of your own town or village.”
“The student may notice known objects in the following list.”“You’ll notice familiar objects in the list below.”

Provide a linguistic map

When the teacher is physically absent, the SIM must be a navigator to the learner; and, without such navigation, he/she may feel lost. To bring in this effect,, you could include signposts, transitional devices, and structural cues.

Signposting uses language to tell the student exactly where they are in their learning journey.

So, instead of saying “The next section covers the Treaty of Versailles”, you could say, “Having discussed the causes of WWI in Section 2.1, we will now examine the Treaty of Versailles to see how the peace process actually sowed the seeds for future conflict.”

Linguistic maps can also prepare the learner for what is coming, reducing the mental effort required to process new data.

“In this module, we will first define X, then contrast it with Y, and finally apply both to a real-world case study.”

They can stimulate a dialogue as well.

Inserting rhetorical questions or directional phrases like, “You might be wondering why we ignored factor Z…” or “Pause here for a moment and look back at Table 4.3.”

Linguistic cues can be used to indicate the changes in tone or gestures of a teacher when emphasising a point.

Using explicit markers like, “Crucially…”, “The most vital takeaway here is…”, or “Note that this formula differs from the one in Session 1.”

The following table will give you a clearer idea about what happens when you use linguistic maps in the SIMs you write.

Capitalisation

In ODL materials, over-capitalisation is a common issue that can actually make it harder for the learner to identify proper nouns or the start of new sentences.

Common nouns vs. proper nouns
Incorrect: The Students must submit their Assignments to the Regional Centre.
Correct: The students must submit their assignments to the Regional Centre.
Why: “Students” and “assignments” are general categories (common nouns), while “Regional Centre” is a specific OUSL entity (proper noun).

Academic subjects and disciplines
Incorrect: I am interested in studying Psychology and Social Sciences.
Correct: I am interested in studying psychology and social sciences.
Exception: “I am enrolled in PLU3301: Introduction to Psychology.” (Here, it is a specific course title).

Titles and roles
Incorrect: You should consult your Tutor or the Senior Lecturer for guidance.
Correct: You should consult your tutor or the senior lecturer for guidance.
Correct (Specific): “Please forward your request to Senior Lecturer Dr. Perera.”

Components of the SIM
Incorrect: Please refer to the Learning Outcomes and Activities in the next Chapter.
Correct: Please refer to the learning outcomes and activities in the next session.
Exception: “As stated in Session 4, the results were…” (Used as a specific label).

Punctuation

In ODL materials, punctuation acts as a “silent tutor”. Since the learners cannot hear your pauses or see your emphasis, punctuation marks must guide them through the text to prevent fatigue and misunderstanding. Precise punctuation is critical because many learners may be reading in their second or third language, and clear punctuation clarifies complex academic arguments.

Serial comma (Oxford comma)
Ambiguous: “The module covers developmental psychology, child health and safety and nutrition.” (Is ‘safety and nutrition’ one combined topic or two separate ones?)
Clear (British): “The module covers developmental psychology, child health, and safety and nutrition.”
Importance: It ensures the learner knows exactly how many distinct topics they need to master.

Using colons and semicolons for logical flow
Semicolons are excellent for linking two closely related ideas without the use of a full stop, but keeping the instructional flow moving.
Correct use: “Do not move on to Activity 4 yet; you must first complete the self-assessment on page 20.”
The Colon: Use it to introduce a list or a defining statement.
“The experiment requires three components: a beaker, distilled water, and a thermometer.”

Hyphens vs. Dashes
Incorrect use of dashes can make a text look cluttered or unprofessional.
Hyphens (-): Use for compound words or modifiers.
Example: “A well-structured session,” or “A second-year student.”
En-dashes (–): Use for ranges (dates, pages, or values).
Example: “Read pages 45–67,” or “The 2024–2025 academic year.”
Em-dashes (—): Used sparingly in SIMs to add an abrupt emphasis or an aside—like this—though ODL writers often prefer brackets or commas for a smoother feel.

Question mark
Since SIMs are essentially written in a conversational tone, question marks are vital for engaging the learner’s meta-cognition.
Example: “Have you ever wondered why some metals rust faster than others? In this section, we will find out.”
Note: Always ensure the question mark is placed inside the quotation marks if the question is part of the quote, but outside if it is part of the main sentence.

  • Align outcomes with assessments and activities
  • Structure by week or topic, with consistent templates

Chapter Eight

Integrating Generative AI into Multimodal Instructional Material Development

Page 1 of 9

Overview

In Open and Distance Learning, the course material effectively is the teaching. Learners lean on it to build knowledge and skills largely on their own. Generative AI gives you a new way to support that work. It can draft explanations, generate examples, design activities, and tighten up unclear writing, so a lesson rarely has to start from a blank page.

Its role is to assist, not to replace you. You still decide the learning outcomes, the content, and whether the finished material meets institutional standards. Treat anything it produces as a first draft: something to check carefully before it becomes part of the official course material.

  • Explain the role of generative AI in developing ODL course materials, together with its benefits and its limitations.
  • Apply prompt engineering techniques, including role, contextual, few-shot, iterative, and chained prompting, to generate course content.
  • Use generative AI to plan lessons and to draft explanations, worked examples, case studies, and learner activities.
  • Generate formative assessment items and supporting visual or multimedia resources with the assistance of generative AI.
  • Select an appropriate generative AI tool for a given stage of course development.
  • Critically review AI-generated content for academic accuracy, pedagogical appropriateness, and alignment with learning outcomes.
  • Apply institutional quality assurance principles when incorporating AI-generated material into official course materials.

Key Concepts

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter that are specific to working with generative AI.

Generative Artificial Intelligence

An AI system that produces new text, images, presentations, or other content in response to user instructions, drawing on patterns learned from large collections of information.

Prompt

The instruction given to a generative AI system describing the task, the intended audience, the educational context, and the expected format of the response.

Prompt Engineering

The practice of designing prompts deliberately and iteratively so the system produces accurate, relevant, and educationally meaningful output.

Iterative Prompting

Building a lesson through a sequence of smaller prompts, each refining or extending the previous output, rather than requesting the whole lesson at once.

Prompt Chaining

An extension of iterative prompting in which the output of one prompt is deliberately fed into the next, so outline, explanation, examples, and activities form a connected sequence.

Academic Validation

The process by which an academic reviews AI-generated content for technical accuracy, pedagogical appropriateness, and curriculum alignment before use.

AI Hallucination

An instance in which a generative AI system produces information, a reference, or a claim that sounds plausible and is stated with confidence, but is inaccurate or fabricated.

Good ODL material does more than present information. A learner studying alone needs to apply concepts, reflect on their own understanding, and connect theory to practice. This is why worked examples, activities, case studies, and assessments belong in almost every lesson. This is one area where generative AI genuinely earns its keep: it is good at producing a full set of these from a single prompt, which helps you build a complete, consistent learning unit without losing a whole afternoon to it.

Planning is one of the stages worth getting right early, since a well-sequenced lesson gives a learner a clear path through it. Generative AI can suggest alternative sequences, such as a problem-based structure instead of a purely linear one, and flag prerequisite knowledge a learner needs before tackling something more advanced. Worth a look, even if you do not take the suggestion exactly as written.

It can also help sharpen learning outcomes, suggesting measurable action verbs and flagging outcomes that have crept beyond what the lesson can realistically achieve. As always, check its suggestions against your actual curriculum and cohort before you commit to them.

Lesson outline prompt
You are an instructional designer at the Open University of Sri Lanka. Develop a lesson for first-year undergraduate students studying through Open and Distance Learning. The lesson should include an introduction, clearly stated learning outcomes, learner-friendly explanations, worked examples, reflection questions, self-assessment activities, and a concise summary. Use clear academic language suitable for independent learners.
Planning activity

Select one lesson you are developing or revising. Generate a lesson outline including an introduction, learning outcomes, main topics, learner activities, and self-assessment questions. Compare it with your existing plan and revise before proceeding.

Once the structure is set, generative AI can help with the content itself. Its biggest strength here is adapting an explanation to the audience: give it the learner profile, level of study, and outcome, and the same concept can be pitched quite differently for a postgraduate than for a first-year undergraduate, without losing accuracy along the way.

This is especially useful against writer's block. Ask for a few alternative introductions or summaries and adapt whichever one fits best. What you get back depends heavily on what you put in, though, so be specific: subject, audience, style, length, and any instructional elements you want included.

It is also good at generating examples and analogies that make an abstract idea concrete. Adapt the examples to reflect local industries where you can, and check any analogy carefully, since a neat comparison that is slightly wrong can plant a misconception rather than clear one up.

Content development prompt
You are an experienced instructional designer developing course materials for undergraduate students studying through Open and Distance Learning. Prepare a lesson on Database Normalisation suitable for first-year learners with limited prior knowledge. The lesson should include an engaging introduction, measurable learning outcomes, learner-friendly explanations, two worked examples, one real-world case study, a reflection activity, three self-assessment questions with answers, and a concise summary.
Good practice

Ask the system to generate learning experiences rather than content alone: explanations plus examples, activities, reflection questions, and assessment tasks together, not one at a time.

Worked examples show a learner how a theoretical concept plays out in practice. In an ODL setting, where most learners are working through the material alone, a well-built example does a lot of the heavy lifting. It walks through the reasoning before a learner is asked to try something similar unassisted. Generative AI can turn out several examples across different contexts quickly, so you can pick the strongest rather than settling for the first one you thought of.

For example, a lesson on database normalisation could draw examples from a student registration system, a hospital information system, or a banking application. Reviewing several allows the academic to choose the one that best supports the learning outcomes and the local context.

Worked example prompt
Develop a worked example demonstrating Second Normal Form (2NF) using a university student registration database. Explain each step clearly, identify partial dependencies, and show how the tables are transformed into Second Normal Form. The explanation should be suitable for first-year undergraduate students studying independently.

Case studies push learners to apply theory to a realistic situation. Where a worked example shows how a problem is solved, a case study asks the learner to analyse the situation themselves, weigh up alternative solutions, and justify what they would choose. Generative AI can put together authentic-feeling scenarios drawn from professional practice across a wide range of disciplines.

An education course might examine technology-enhanced learning in a rural school; a management course might examine organisational change in a public sector institution. These scenarios build critical thinking and decision-making skills, but academics should review them carefully to ensure they reflect local professional practice.

Activities exist to get a learner interacting with the material, not just reading it. Generative AI can turn out a good range of these: reflection prompts, concept-mapping exercises, problem-solving tasks, and discussion questions for an online forum, all aimed at keeping an independent learner engaged rather than passive. You can also ask it to work through Bloom's Taxonomy deliberately, so activities build higher-order thinking rather than stopping at recall.

Rather than placing all activities at the end of a lesson, consider distributing shorter activities throughout the content, giving learners regular opportunities to pause and consolidate understanding.

Learner activity prompt
Generate three learner-centred activities for a lesson on cybersecurity. Include one reflection activity, one problem-solving activity, and one collaborative online discussion task suitable for undergraduate students studying through Open and Distance Learning.

Self-assessment lets a learner check their own progress without waiting for a tutor. Generative AI can produce a good spread of formats: multiple-choice, true or false, matching exercises, short answers, and scenario-based problems, each with an explanation of why an answer is right or wrong, not just a mark.

Figure 5

Assessment questions should always be reviewed carefully: verify technical accuracy, ensure only one option is clearly correct where appropriate, and confirm that each question measures the intended learning outcome rather than simple memorisation.

A good visual can do in a glance what a paragraph takes a page to explain. Generative AI can suggest an appropriate diagram, write a description you can hand to an illustrator, or point you toward a sensible way of representing a concept visually. Tools like Napkin AI will turn a written explanation into a simple concept diagram, and Canva Magic Design or Gamma can generate slide layouts from a text prompt. These are useful design assistants, but not a substitute for your own judgement about what the visual should actually show.

The same idea carries over into multimedia. Generative AI can help with video scripts, narration, podcast outlines, and storyboard ideas. Suppose you have just finished a lesson on network security. You could ask for a five-minute video script summarising the key concepts, then adapt and record it yourself as a supplementary resource.

4.8 Prompt Engineering

The quality of what you get back depends almost entirely on the quality of what you put in. A strong prompt usually combines a handful of elements: the role you want the AI to adopt, who the learners are, the educational context, the outcomes you are aiming for, the writing style, and the format you expect. A walkthrough of building strong prompts, covering much the same ground as the techniques below.

Give the AI a specific identity, such as an instructional designer or a subject matter expert, before you ask it for anything. That one step nudges it toward an educational register instead of a generic, encyclopaedia-style answer.

Prompt
You are an experienced instructional designer at the Open University of Sri Lanka. Develop a lesson on Object-Oriented Programming for first-year undergraduate students studying through Open and Distance Learning. The lesson should include an introduction, measurable learning outcomes, learner-friendly explanations, worked examples, learner activities, self-assessment questions, and a summary.

Tell it about your learners and the setting before you ask for content. Do not assume it already knows your audience. Prior knowledge, course level, expected duration, and any institutional requirements: the more specific the brief, the less revising you will usually need to do afterward.

Prompt
Prepare a lesson on cloud computing for first-year undergraduate students studying through Open and Distance Learning. Assume that learners have basic computer literacy but no previous knowledge of cloud technologies. Explain concepts using simple language, practical examples, short paragraphs, and frequent opportunities for learner reflection.

Show the AI one or two examples of what you want rather than describing it in the abstract. This earns its keep especially when several people are contributing to the same course and need to match an existing style.

Prompt
The following lesson illustrates the structure used throughout this course. Analyse its organisation and writing style, then develop a new lesson on Relational Database Management Systems using the same structure, level of detail, and writing style.

Build the lesson through a series of smaller prompts rather than one large request, refining and expanding it at each stage. A typical sequence looks like this:

  • Step 1: Generate the lesson outline.
  • Step 2: Expand each lesson section.
  • Step 3: Develop worked examples.
  • Step 4: Generate learner activities.
  • Step 5: Create formative assessment questions.
  • Step 6: Write the lesson summary.
  • Step 7: Review the lesson for clarity and consistency.

Prompt chaining takes iterative prompting a step further by feeding the output of one prompt directly into the next: an outline becomes the input for the detailed explanations, which become the basis for the examples, then the activities and assessments. It mirrors how you would actually write a course by hand, and it tends to produce more coherent material than asking for everything in one go.

Prompt engineering activity

Choose one lesson you are preparing and develop it using each technique in turn: a simple prompt first, then role, contextual, few-shot, and iterative. Compare the outputs and reflect on which technique produced the most useful results.

Good practice

Good prompt engineering is rarely a single interaction. Expect to go back and refine a prompt several times before what comes out is actually ready for your course materials.

Quality Assurance

Generative AI can speed up drafting considerably, but the responsibility for academic quality stays with you, in full, at every stage covered in this chapter.

Figure 9
The Draft, Review, Revise, Approve Cycle
Note. Every AI-assisted lesson passes through this loop before it reaches a learner. A draft that fails review returns for revision rather than moving forward to approval.

Treat anything AI-generated as a draft that still needs your review, not a finished product. Check the technical accuracy, make sure it lines up with the learning outcomes, confirm the examples actually fit, and adjust the language where it needs it, so the whole course reads consistently.

Generative AI is there to support your writing, not stand in for it. The strongest course materials combine an AI-generated draft with your own subject expertise, teaching experience, and sense of what your learners need, then get reviewed for accuracy, consistency, accessibility, and fit with institutional standards before they go anywhere near a student.

AI systems will occasionally get things wrong: inaccurate information, a missing concept, or an example that does not quite fit the learning context. References are worth double-checking too, since these are sometimes invented outright. Read every AI-generated output with a critical eye before it becomes part of the official course material.

Quality checklist
  • Have all facts, figures, and technical claims been verified against a trusted source?
  • Do the learning outcomes, explanations, examples, and assessment items align with one another?
  • Are the examples and case studies appropriate for the intended learners, and localised where relevant?
  • Has every reference or citation produced by the AI been checked, since these can be fabricated?
  • Does the language, tone, and level of detail match the rest of the course material?
  • Has the content been reviewed against institutional quality standards before publication?

Summary

Generative AI has become a genuinely useful resource for developing ODL course materials. Used thoughtfully, it can help you plan lessons, write learner-centred explanations, put together worked examples and case studies, design activities, prepare formative assessments, tighten up unclear writing, and create supporting multimedia. Good prompt engineering gets you more relevant, more accurate output, and picking the right tool for each stage saves you the time you would otherwise spend fighting the wrong one.

The responsibility for academic quality stays with you. Combine generative AI with sound instructional design and your own professional judgement, and you can put together engaging, learner-centred course materials that genuinely support independent learning, not material that merely looks finished.

Closing

Conclusion

This handbook opened with the foundations of Open and Distance Learning and the study session as the basic unit of self-instruction. From there it moved outward, into the different modes through which material can reach a learner, the process a course team moves through to turn an outcome into a finished session, and then into the individual crafts of writing for print, producing audio-visual material, and building a course inside a learning management system. It closes here, with the newest addition to a course writer's toolkit: generative AI, and how to use it to support your work rather than take it over.

Eight chapters, eight contributors, and a fairly wide range of formats and tools. Underneath all of it sits the same small set of commitments. Material designed around the learner, not the convenience of whoever's writing it. Outcomes stated clearly enough to guide every decision that follows. And a writer who stays responsible for the final word, whatever helped draft it along the way.

None of this is meant to be read once and shelved. The comparison tables, prompt templates, activities, and checklists throughout this book are built to be returned to each time you sit down to plan a new session or revise an old one. Treat it as a working reference rather than a cover-to-cover read.

Course writing at a distance keeps changing, and this handbook will need to change with it. If something here stops working for you, or a better way emerges, that is worth raising with CETMe so the next revision can reflect it, just as this edition was built on the one before it.
